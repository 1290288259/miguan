A B S T R A C T

[p1_b8] page=1 bbox=[202.0, 288.27, 559.79, 414.74]
As cyberattacks get more sophisticated, security systems must learn to detect and deceive them. DecoyPot, a
honeypot Web Application Programming Interface (API) that generates legitimate API responses, is introduced in
this paper. DecoyPot’s command extractor module carefully analyzes API requests to create prompt-response
pairs that improve a Retrieval-Augmented Generation based (RAG) large language model (LLM). DecoyPot
can instantly adjust its answers to mimic API activity in a contextually correct and convincing manner to at­
tackers. To assess system efficacy, we used a two-phase similarity analysis. Initial queries were matched with
prompt-response pairs to ensure contextually suitable responses. Second, similarity measures were used to
compare generated responses to reference responses, producing an average score of 0.9780. The high score shows
that the system can create API-like responses, boosting its utility. DecoyPot engaged opponents and learned their
Tactics, Techniques and Procedures (TTPs). The study shows that honeypot cybersecurity effectiveness must be
improved by merging AI-driven response creation with enhanced deception technologies. DecoyPot effectively
adapts to incoming queries and generates API-like responses, delivering actionable cyber threat intelligence and
enhancing proactive defense strategies.

1. Introduction

[p1_b10] page=1 bbox=[37.59, 462.66, 291.03, 580.29]
Over the last couple of decades, the digital landscape has completely
changed, and cyberattacks have increased in all sectors. These range
from small organizations to critical infrastructure, exploiting vulnera­
bilities brought about by the proliferation of connected devices. This
rapid growth of attack surfaces automatically demands innovation and
adaptiveness in cybersecurity measures. One critical tool among these is
the honeypot. Honeypots operate by emulating vulnerable systems to
attract attackers and divert their focus from real targets, thereby
revealing their techniques. This type of interaction generates valuable
intelligence that informs the security strategy, discloses patterns of
attack, and enables countermeasure development.

[p1_b11] page=1 bbox=[37.59, 577.69, 291.02, 653.54]
Cybersecurity honeypots have evolved significantly from their initial
designs, which were limited to static systems aimed at identifying and
logging basic network activity. While these early implementations pro­
vided valuable insights into basic attacker behaviors, they were insuf­
ficient for addressing sophisticated threats, such as advanced persistent
threats (APTs). Modern honeypots have transitioned into dynamic and
interactive systems that simulate realistic environments, enabling real-

[p1_b12] page=1 bbox=[306.59, 441.74, 560.02, 507.05]
time engagement with attackers and capturing detailed information
about their tactics, techniques, and procedures. Despite these advance­
ments, high-interaction honeypots present challenges, including
increased complexity, resource demands, and the need for strict isola­
tion to avoid potential risks to production environments (Kristyanto
et al., 2022).

[p1_b13] page=1 bbox=[306.59, 504.5, 560.02, 653.54]
Despite their advancements, traditional honeypots face significant
challenges in adapting to the rapidly evolving tactics of modern at­
tackers. Static or poorly configured systems are particularly vulnerable
to detection, causing attackers to disengage or, worse, exploit the hon­
eypot to mislead defenders. High-interaction honeypots, while more
effective at engaging attackers, generate large volumes of data that
require time-intensive manual analysis, leading to response delays and
missed opportunities for threat mitigation. Furthermore, many organi­
zations struggle to operationalize the intelligence gathered from these
systems, limiting their overall utility (Jiang and Zheng, 2020). Tradi­
tional honeypots often fail to attract sophisticated attackers, such as
state-sponsored adversaries, due to their lack of dynamic interaction,
which restricts their ability to gather actionable intelligence on attacker
motivations and goals.

Integration of artificial intelligence (AI) and machine learning (ML)
in honeypot architecture has brought a sea change in the traditional
designs that were usually challenged by many drawbacks. This is ach­
ieved by utilizing AI for network traffic analysis and the identification of
malicious behavior, followed by adaptive adjustment of their responses
to keep the attackers engaged. The capabilities would make honeypots
more resilient and increase their efficacy in detecting and countering
complex threats. Machine learning allows honeypots to filter out back­
ground noise and irrelevant data efficiently, thus focusing on real
threats. This reduces the workload for analysts, accelerates detection
and response, and overall reinforces the cybersecurity framework
(Angeli et al., 2024).

[p2_b2] page=2 bbox=[37.59, 176.82, 291.03, 325.85]
Recent advancements have enhanced honeypots by integrating large
language models, including GPT-3. This model allows honeypots to
interact with adversaries in a very human-like way, therefore luring
them into exposing their tactics, techniques, and procedures. These in­
teractions are particularly useful for responding to social engineering
attacks, where the adversary leverages human vulnerabilities to gain
unauthorized access to systems. In such cases, LLM-enhanced honeypots
gather valuable intelligence on attacker tactics and motivations by
generating realistic dialogues (Raut et al., 2023). The latest generation
of honeypots, equipped with retrieval-augmented generation tech­
niques, offers greater dynamism and adaptability. RAG allows honeypot
systems to achieve a far better level of contextual accuracy and
responsiveness by leveraging external data sources for real-time adap­
tation to diversified attack patterns and conditions.

[p2_b3] page=2 bbox=[37.59, 323.31, 291.03, 472.29]
The integration of RAG and LLMs brings substantial novelties to
honeypot design: first, RAG frameworks allow the honeypot to fetch
relevant external data with which to generate contextually appropriate
responses, enhancing the realism in emulating a real-world environment
(Dang et al., 2024). In a honeypot, it enhances the reality enough to
make it credulously sustainable for extended interaction with attackers.
Similarly, LLM allows for complex and dynamic responses that keep
pace with adversaries’ expected actions strongly. In the context of web
application honeypots, advanced technologies have been utilized in
emulating API operations in an efficient manner while their outcomes
are based on contextual information. Therefore, combining RAG and
LLMs arms honeypots with a capability of high-order deceiving while
harvesting critical intelligence of attacker’s techniques and strategic
mindsets (Buzzio-Garcia, 2021).

[p2_b4] page=2 bbox=[37.59, 469.75, 291.02, 618.73]
This paper presents a new honeypot system using large language
models with retrieval-augmented generation to mimic the conversations
involved in Web APIs. The first part outlines two important components:
the command extractor and the response generator. The command
extractor converts the API requests into structured prompt-response
pairs for enriching the fine-tuning of the large language model. In
contrast, a response generator augmented with out-of-the-box knowl­
edge from retrieval-augmented generation would simulate realistic and
contextually relevant responses to engage attackers. The system was
evaluated using a best-match similarity metric, whereby the inputs from
attackers were compared with outputs produced by the LLM. The test
shows that the honeypot is able to realistically emulate a real system and
attract malicious actors, providing real-world information to improve
security measures.

[p2_b5] page=2 bbox=[37.59, 616.18, 291.03, 744.3]
This study introduces DecoyPot, a novel honeypot system that le­
verages state-of-the-art technologies, including Retrieval-Augmented
Generation and Large Language Models, to create dynamic and con­
textually accurate API interactions. Unlike traditional honeypots,
DecoyPot combines generative modeling with retrieval mechanisms to
enhance deception capabilities and adapt to sophisticated attacker
strategies. The system’s effectiveness has been demonstrated through
rigorous evaluations, achieving high-response similarity scores with real
API interactions. This performance highlights DecoyPot’s ability to
generate realistic and semantically aligned responses, ensuring a
convincing engagement with attackers while collecting valuable intel­
ligence on their tactics and techniques. DecoyPot’s architecture has been

[p2_b6] page=2 bbox=[306.59, 51.3, 559.99, 95.74]
validated across diverse API domains, including Internet of Things,
finance, healthcare, and e-commerce. This adaptability underscores its
versatility in addressing various attack vectors, making it a robust tool
for proactive cyber defense and deception.

2. Related work

[p2_b9] page=2 bbox=[306.59, 229.2, 560.02, 325.91]
From very simple decoy mechanisms to more sophisticated,
dynamically adapting, intelligent platforms, honeypots have evolved
into critical components of modern cybersecurity methodologies.
Effectively attracting, interacting with, and misleading attackers, hon­
eypots provide the precious intelligence about malicious strategies be­
sides functioning as an important layer in security. The section is focused
on honeypot technology evolution through detailed application strate­
gies, optimization techniques, and applications across all main areas of
cybersecurity.

[p2_b10] page=2 bbox=[306.59, 334.08, 410.56, 347.11]
2.1. Introduction to honeypots

[p2_b11] page=2 bbox=[306.59, 355.0, 560.01, 451.71]
Honeypots are security mechanisms that mimic a vulnerable system
with the intention of attracting an attacker, studying their behavior, and
gathering relevant intelligence. The first honeypots were very simple
and static structures that mimicked a common vulnerability, but they
proved to be no match against the ever-evolving sophistication of cyber
threats. While these primitive honeypots were good enough for simple
reconnaissance, their utility quickly faded as attackers developed tools
that could identify and avoid these traps (Kristyanto et al., 2022, Angeli
et al., 2024, Maldonado et al., 2024).

[p2_b12] page=2 bbox=[306.59, 449.17, 560.02, 535.44]
Such shortcomings were, until recently, countered by new honeypot
technologies based ML, AI, and LLMs. These enhancements further
allowed the honeypot to dynamically adapt to the new methods of
attack, simulate real environments, and interact more effectively with
an attacker. The evolution has so far expanded their use beyond the
traditional network security to also involve those on Internet of Things
(IoT), Industrial Control Systems (ICS), and Cyber-physical systems
(CPS) applications (Sladi´c et al., 2024, Suhail et al., 2023).

[p2_b13] page=2 bbox=[306.59, 543.62, 426.1, 556.65]
2.2. Interaction levels in honeypots

[p2_b14] page=2 bbox=[306.59, 564.54, 560.02, 682.16]
Low-interaction honeypots can mimic very basic functionalities,
such as simple network services, to attract attackers. These systems are
generally lightweight and very easy to deploy, and hence suitable for
detecting reconnaissance activities (Kristyanto et al., 2022). However,
their limited interaction capabilities often fail to deceive sophisticated
attackers who may identify them as decoys. The study in (Lygerou et al.,
2022) illustrates the use of low-interaction honeypots in IoT networks.
Such systems emulate protocol-specific environments to monitor IoT
traffic to detect potential threats (Karpukhin et al., 2020). Although
useful for the initial detection stage, low-interaction honeypots cannot
provide an insight into advanced attack strategies.

[p2_b15] page=2 bbox=[306.59, 679.56, 560.02, 744.92]
Medium-interaction honeypots provide an effective compromise
between functionality and resource efficiency by emulating services
such as databases or web servers. These systems are particularly suitable
for environments with moderate security requirements, as they balance
engagement depth with ease of deployment and maintenance
(Karpukhin et al., 2020, Lewis et al., 2020). By enabling attackers to

detailed intelligence on attacker methods and tools. As an example, a
database honeypot emulating a MySQL server would reveal very valu­

insightful tool in cybersecurity strategies.

[p3_b2] page=3 bbox=[37.59, 176.82, 291.02, 294.45]
High-interaction honeypots emulate whole systems, allowing the
attacker free rein to interact with a realistic environment. These are
resource-intensive systems but very valuable for research into advanced
threats in critical infrastructure sectors such as ICS (Lygerou et al., 2022,
Rashid et al., 2024). In high-stakes environments like ICS,
high-interaction honeypots must accurately replicate industrial pro­
cesses to gather actionable intelligence on attacker behavior. For
example, works like (Lygerou et al., 2022) consider using
game-theoretic models to optimize the deployment of high-interaction
honeypots while ensuring efficient resource allocation for maximizing
threat data collection.

[p3_b3] page=3 bbox=[37.59, 302.67, 128.04, 315.7]
2.3. Deployment strategies

[p3_b4] page=3 bbox=[37.59, 323.59, 290.98, 357.54]
Deployment has significantly changed with honeypots, reflecting the
growing complexity of the threat landscape. Advances in deployment
strategies have made them more effective and adaptive.

[p3_b5] page=3 bbox=[37.59, 355.0, 291.03, 430.79]
Traditional honeypots are static deployments that emulate vulnera­
bilities for the purpose of collecting intelligence. They actually fare quite
well in monitoring malicious activity in an effort to improve overall
network security measures (Kristyanto et al., 2022, Angeli et al., 2024,
Maldonado et al., 2024). While effective against simple threats, tradi­
tional honeypots have some limitations due to their inability to
dynamically react to new attack vectors.

[p3_b6] page=3 bbox=[37.59, 428.19, 291.02, 535.39]
Adaptive honeypots are a giant leap forward, integrating ML and AI
in analyzing incoming traffic and dynamically adjusting responses. This
learning from attacker behavior extends the engagement time and im­
proves intelligence collection (Gillick et al., 2019). The idea of rein­
forcement learning, for example, can enable honeypots to adjust their
operation with respect to the behavior being observed by an attacker.
This is useful, especially in complex attack environments such as en­
terprise networks and IoT ecosystems. There is a need to keep their
pertinence in response to evolving cyber threats by using adaptive
honeypots.

[p3_b7] page=3 bbox=[37.59, 532.79, 291.02, 608.63]
Specialized honeypots are thus designed to meet very specific secu­
rity challenges within certain environments. For example, (Zhou et al.,
2023) uses game-theoretic methods to optimize the deployment of
honeypots within ICS environments. Similarly, honeypots in the
cloud-as discussed in (Rashid et al., 2024), scalable solutions that
monitor industrial networks and further secure Industry 4.0
infrastructures.

[p3_b8] page=3 bbox=[37.59, 616.81, 180.85, 629.84]
2.4. Optimization techniques in honeypots

[p3_b9] page=3 bbox=[37.59, 637.73, 291.02, 744.92]
Machine learning and AI have transformed honeypots into systems
that can analyze huge amounts of data, identify patterns, and respond on
a real-time basis. This enhances the detection capability and reduces
manual effort in monitoring and analysis. For example, in the web-based
honeypot in (Jiang and Zheng, 2020), ensemble ML techniques are
applied to filter out noise and focus on significant threats for improving
the accuracy of detection. Similarly, Generative Adversarial Networks
(GANs), applied in (Ding, 2024), optimize honeypot responses, giving
them greater effectiveness in resource-constrained environments.
AI-powered honeypots largely improve the level of interaction by

[p3_b10] page=3 bbox=[306.59, 51.3, 560.01, 95.74]
emulating human-like interactive capabilities that enable effective
deception and intelligence gathering. Realistic responses of generative
AI models in (Raut et al., 2023), let honeypots dynamically learn from
and adapt their tactics to attackers.

[p3_b11] page=3 bbox=[306.59, 93.14, 560.02, 263.09]
The deployment of honeypots is optimized using behavioral intelli­
gence from the SOAR engine (Bartwal et al., 2022), analyzing attacker
behaviors to guide strategic placement. When deployed in response to
specific threats, these honeypots achieved longer engagement times and
captured much more information about attacker tactics. These SOAR
findings, in combination with the capabilities of LLMs, make this hon­
eypot especially relevant to modern contexts via the capability of per­
forming and dynamically updating responses in web API settings. The
approaches described in (Li et al., 2024) use RAG to increase the realism
of
honeypot interactions. The RAG-based approach retrieves
pre-existing responses from a knowledge base and uses them as
contextual inputs during the generation process, enabling the honeypot
to correctly mimic the behaviors of legitimate APIs. Combining RAG
with LLMs ensures that the responses of the honeypot are contextually
accurate and semantically aligned, making it much more effective in
engaging and deceiving attackers.

[p3_b12] page=3 bbox=[306.59, 260.49, 560.01, 420.02]
Reinforcement learning is now one of the essential tools in opti­
mizing honeypot functionality in dynamically adjusting the behaviors of
an attacker, thereby further extending the durations of engagement.
This adaptability enables the honeypot to harvest full intelligence about
tactics employed by attackers and raises the utility value of use in the
context of cybersecurity dramatically. In this context, (Lopez-Yepez and
Fagette, 2022) in the dynamic scenario, dynamic Secure Shell (SSH)
honeypots make use of deep reinforcement learning methods for them to
become sensitive, intelligently reacting to the behavior of detected at­
tackers. Not only does this customized approach increase the attacker’s
time of interaction, but it also improves the honeypot ability of col­
lecting vital information and learning new attack schemes. Building on
that idea, (Gao et al., 2024) presented DShopping, an intelligent
deception system to assist in fighting APTs by relying on exploiting static
security configurations.

[p3_b13] page=3 bbox=[306.59, 417.42, 560.02, 618.73]
By using reinforcement learning, DSHopping is able to dynamically
change the scenarios of deception for optimized engagement of attackers
and real-time detection of APT behaviors. It underlines not only the
synergy between machine learning and honeypot systems but also how
they grow and react against complex and variable attack vectors.
Through dynamic adaptation to adversaries’ strategies, the effectiveness
of reinforcement learning—powered honeypots is enhanced several
times over, while simultaneously contributing exponentially to cyber­
security intelligence. Hybrid AI architectures, fusing deep reinforcement
learning, rule-based models, and LLMs, enable further evolution in
reinforcing autonomous cybersecurity defense. These architectures, in
their basic form, deploy agents in simulated environments that learn to
interact with the main purpose of critical network protection by
continuously enhancing their capability to predict attacks and response;
an example of the adaptability of modern AI-driven honeypots. The
incorporation of LLMs within such a hybrid architecture implies that
honeypots can enhance the quality of response by better exploiting
adaptive learning informed by intricate attack patterns (Loevenich et al.,
2024).

2.5. Applications in advanced domains

[p4_b3] page=4 bbox=[37.59, 386.69, 291.03, 483.4]
IoT devices are very susceptible to cyberattacks because of their
weak security features and easy availability. IoT network-based hon­
eypots use deception technologies in analyzing attackers’ behavior for
improving the security of devices. Various works, such as (Sharma et al.,
2023) and (Qin et al., 2023) present the utilization of IoT-based hon­
eypots for monitoring traffic and detecting threats that target specific
protocols. Decentralized honeypots have been deployed in (Karpukhin
et al., 2020), to emulate protocol-specific environments, thereby gaining
insights into attack patterns and improving IoT security.

[p4_b4] page=4 bbox=[37.59, 480.86, 291.03, 577.57]
Cyber-physical systems combine physical processes with computa­
tional systems and thus are a very significant target for sophisticated
cyber-attacks. Honeypots in CPS environments are an essential tool in
the study and mitigation of such threats. The research in (Tian et al.,
2019), puts forth a model that strikes a balance between low- and
high-interaction honeypots toward effectively protecting CPS networks.
Further, game-theoretic approaches in (Zhou et al., 2023) optimize
honeypot deployment in ICS environments toward ensuring efficient use
of the honeypot resources while raising detection rates.

[p4_b5] page=4 bbox=[37.59, 574.97, 291.03, 650.76]
Unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) rely on interconnected systems,
making them susceptible to cyber threats. Honeypots designed for UAV
networks mitigate denial-of-service (DoS) attacks by simulating
attacker-defender interactions using game theory (Miao and Li ve Q.
Pan, 2023). Cloud-based honeypots, as explored in (Rashid et al., 2024),
provide real-time insights into threats targeting UAV systems, enhancing
their resilience against cyberattacks.

[p4_b6] page=4 bbox=[37.59, 648.22, 291.01, 703.09]
The integration of honeypots and AI-driven IDS in IoT blockchain
networks enhances their overall security. In (Commey et al., 2024), re­
searchers employed Bayesian game theory combined with reinforce­
ment learning to dynamically adapt honeypots to the attacker behavior,
thus ensuring robust proactive defenses.

[p4_b7] page=4 bbox=[37.59, 700.49, 291.03, 744.93]
Very specialized honeypots are needed in protection ICS environ­
ments, since those complex systems under study must be emulated. The
importance of high-interaction honeypots, explained in detail in (Zhou
et al., 2023), allows deeper studies on attacks against ICS and shows how

[p4_b8] page=4 bbox=[306.59, 282.49, 560.02, 441.96]
the most sophisticated attacks can be realized by advanced adversaries.
Honeypots of this nature imitate entire systems, enabling extended in­
teractions with attackers, which opens up the possibility for researchers
to gather large amounts of data regarding the methodologies, strategies,
and processes involved in attacks. In addition, as demonstrated in
(Rashid et al., 2024), the scalability and flexibility of cloud-based hon­
eypots make them more useful in industrial environments since they can
easily be deployed in different environments. The price paid for such
effectiveness in the study of cyberattacks against critical infrastructures
is a high resource cost. High-interaction honeypots require detailed
configuration to emulate real-world ICS environments, hence their
deployment is resource-intensive and technically challenging (Conti
et al., 2022). While they are technically difficult to deploy and maintain,
due to the fact that they can provide detailed intelligence on cyber
threats, they are indispensable in securing critical industrial systems.

3. System design and overview

[p4_b10] page=4 bbox=[306.59, 472.7, 560.02, 558.97]
The decoy Web API honeypot system uses a Retrieval-Augmented
Generation framework paired with a fine-tuned large language model
to generate responses that are contextually appropriate and semantically
plausible. In this architectural design, the system tries to mimic real API
functionality as much as possible in order to mislead potential attackers
while harvesting valuable information about their strategies. The system
has modular architecture, which improves the automation, scalability,
and accuracy of the simulation of the API interactions.

[p4_b11] page=4 bbox=[306.59, 574.97, 468.57, 588.0]
3.1. Retrieval-augmented generation framework

[p4_b12] page=4 bbox=[306.59, 595.89, 560.01, 692.6]
RAG is a hybrid model: It combines the precision of retrieval-based
systems with the flexibility of generative modeling. Traditional gener­
ative models, while impressive in terms of generating human-like text,
are generally constrained to patterns learned during training and can
therefore easily respond with out-of-context or irrelevant answers; RAG
overcomes this limitation. Thus, it uses dynamic retrieval of relevant
information from an external knowledge source to ground the generated

4.4. Evaluation of similarity scores

[p6_b23] page=6 bbox=[306.59, 418.04, 560.01, 504.32]
Similarity score evaluation plays a critical role in ensuring semantic
alignment between the honeypot-generated responses and the legiti­
mate API behaviors. This process measures the fidelity of the generated
outputs by quantifying their alignment with documented responses,
hence validating the system’s capability to produce realistic in­
teractions. The similarity scores are computed using cosine similarity, a
mathematical approach that determines angular closeness between two
high-dimensional vector representations of text.

[p6_b24] page=6 bbox=[306.59, 501.78, 560.02, 598.49]
To calculate these scores, the generated response and the docu­
mented API response are first passed through the transformer-based
embedding model to obtain their dense vector representations, which
capture the semantic relationships between words and phrases. The
embeddings for each text are extracted by averaging the token embed­
dings from the last hidden layer of the transformer, yielding one vector
that represents the meaning of the whole text. This embedding step
helps the system capture subtle context that is important for evaluating
API behavior.

[p6_b25] page=6 bbox=[306.59, 595.89, 560.02, 713.51]
Once embeddings have been generated, the next step is to calculate
cosine similarity. This metric calculates the cosine of the angle between
two vectors, returning a scalar value within the range of −1 to 1. The
closer the value is to 1, the higher the semantic similarity. A value close
to 0 means there is no significant relation. In this application, negative
similarity scores are few and generally represent contrasting meanings,
which are unlikely in API response generation. The formula for this
calculation will involve the dot product of the two vectors in the
numerator, divided by the magnitude of the two vectors multiplied
together in the denominator. This normalizes and ensures that the
similarity is scale-independent of the absolute embeddings.

[p6_b26] page=6 bbox=[306.59, 710.97, 559.98, 744.92]
Similarity test includes the comparison between the embeddings of
the generated response and pre-computed embedding of all entries in
the knowledge base. Comparisons give a similarity score, and the one

incoming request and the stored prompts. When the system receives a
request, it calculates a measure of its similarity relative to all prompts
stored in the prompt-response dataset. The evaluation can be performed
with various measures of similarity, such as cosine similarity or semantic
similarity based on embeddings, which represent contextual corre­
spondence between the incoming request and the archived prompts very
effectively. The trigger with the highest value of similarity is selected
from them, and its corresponding response is used as the reference for
both the response production and evaluation. It then assures the decoy
system obtains a reference response that optimally matches the intent

and context of the incoming question.

[p8_b2] page=8 bbox=[37.59, 219.17, 291.03, 347.28]
During the second stage, Response Similarity Evaluation, the quality

selected prompt-response pair. The goal of this verification is to check

(2) the comparison between the generated response and the actual
response associated with the matched prompt-response pair. Table 1
provides a summary of sample requests, detailing the request similarity
scores, reference responses, generated responses, and response similar­
ity scores.

[p8_b5] page=8 bbox=[37.59, 470.37, 291.04, 661.25]
For every incoming request, the system calculates a Request Simi­
larity score, matching it against previously stored ones in the dataset, to
select the prompt-response pair having the highest degree of match. This
selection shows the systems’ ability to correctly single out contextually
relevant prompts. As a case in point, the GET request with a similarity
score of 0.9887 was matched with a saved request that fetches trans­
action details, demonstrating that the system was able to recognize and
align the context of the request in the dataset accurately. The Response
Similarity score quantifies how much the generated response corre­
sponds to the reference response from the relevant prompt-response
pair, ensuring that the simulated API functionality in the context of
IoT home automation is accurate. The system correctly emulates ex­
pected API outputs, as evidenced by the similarity scores of 1.0000 for
POST adding a new smart device and 0.9992 for DELETE removing a
user’s profile. These results confirm this system in its capability of
creating realistic yet contextually exact decoy responses to impersonate
real API interactions aimed at deceiving attackers and gathering
meaningful intelligence.

[p8_b6] page=8 bbox=[37.59, 658.65, 291.0, 744.92]
An even better illustration of the honeypot’s efficacy in response
generation is demonstrated by the average response similarity score of
0.9780. Such a high average score indicates a system with a reliable
ability to generate responses that closely match the expected results,
which would help it serve more effectively as a powerful deception
mechanism in cybersecurity. By generating replies that are plausible and
contextually relevant, the decoy web API can go much deeper in its
ability to interact with potential attackers, thus revealing a great deal

[p8_b7] page=8 bbox=[306.59, 208.74, 432.4, 221.77]
about their tactics and motivations.

[p8_b8] page=8 bbox=[306.59, 229.65, 360.92, 242.69]
7. Conclusion

[p8_b9] page=8 bbox=[306.59, 250.57, 560.01, 336.85]
This work describes the design and evaluation of a decoy Web API
honeypot based on state-of-the-art language models with Retrieval-
Augmented Generation, for Deriving believable answers and enlarging
its potential of being an effective decoy in the context of cybersecurity.
The architecture is able to obtain contextually appropriate and plausible
responses by adaptively matching incoming requests to the established
prompt-response pairs and evaluating response accuracy by applying
similarity metrics.

[p8_b10] page=8 bbox=[306.59, 334.25, 560.02, 535.56]
These results show that the honeypot is capable of actively inter­
acting with potential attackers, as indicated by the high request and
response similarity scores. With a mean response similarity value of
0.9780, the system proves capable of closely mimicking expected API
behaviors, thereby rendering an authentic user experience that en­
courages a longer stay by users. It has a high level of realism, which not
only deceives the adversaries but also allows gathering important in­
sights about their strategies and methodologies for improving defensive
capabilities. This research delineates the potential benefits of using LLM-
based honeypots in the cybersecurity domain to protect critical systems
by redirecting aggressors away from actual resources and monitoring
their actions in a controlled environment. Future work will continue to
diversify the dataset of prompt-response pairs, allowing for a larger
range of API interactions. Another promising avenue is increasing the
model’s real-time adaptability in order to detect even more complex
forms of malicious behavior. Therefore, integrating state-of-the-art
artificial intelligence into honeypot systems is considered a very prom­
ising direction forward, shaping the future of advanced and proactive
cyber defense solutions.
