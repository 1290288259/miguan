detailed intelligence on attacker methods and tools. As an example, a 
database honeypot emulating a MySQL server would reveal very valu -
able insights into the SQL injection techniques that a hacker may employ 
and yet the resource requirements are manageable. This approach is 
further improved by the LLMs integration, and this is illustrated in ( Hu 
et al., 2024 ), with training LLMs on SQL queries responses. These hon -

appropriately given real-world contexts. This realism not only effec -

tactics, thus making the medium-interaction honeypot a practical and 
insightful tool in cybersecurity strategies.
High-interaction honeypots emulate whole systems, allowing the 
attacker free rein to interact with a realistic environment. These are 
resource-intensive systems but very valuable for research into advanced 
threats in critical infrastructure sectors such as ICS ( Lygerou et al., 2022 , 
Rashid et al., 2024 ). In high-stakes environments like ICS, 
high-interaction honeypots must accurately replicate industrial pro -
cesses to gather actionable intelligence on attacker behavior. For 
example, works like ( Lygerou et al., 2022 ) consider using 
game-theoretic models to optimize the deployment of high-interaction 
honeypots while ensuring efficient resource allocation for maximizing 
threat data collection.
2.3. Deployment strategies
Deployment has significantly changed with honeypots, reflecting the 
growing complexity of the threat landscape. Advances in deployment 
strategies have made them more effective and adaptive.
Traditional honeypots are static deployments that emulate vulnera -
bilities for the purpose of collecting intelligence. They actually fare quite 
well in monitoring malicious activity in an effort to improve overall 
network security measures ( Kristyanto et al., 2022 , Angeli et al., 2024 , 
Maldonado et al., 2024 ). While effective against simple threats, tradi -
tional honeypots have some limitations due to their inability to 
dynamically react to new attack vectors.
Adaptive honeypots are a giant leap forward, integrating ML and AI 
in analyzing incoming traffic and dynamically adjusting responses. This 
learning from attacker behavior extends the engagement time and im -
proves intelligence collection ( Gillick et al., 2019 ). The idea of rein -
forcement learning, for example, can enable honeypots to adjust their 
operation with respect to the behavior being observed by an attacker. 
This is useful, especially in complex attack environments such as en -
terprise networks and IoT ecosystems. There is a need to keep their 
pertinence in response to evolving cyber threats by using adaptive 
honeypots.
Specialized honeypots are thus designed to meet very specific secu -
rity challenges within certain environments. For example, ( Zhou et al., 
2023 ) uses game-theoretic methods to optimize the deployment of 
honeypots within ICS environments. Similarly, honeypots in the 
cloud-as discussed in ( Rashid et al., 2024 ), scalable solutions that 
monitor industrial networks and further secure Industry 4.0 
infrastructures.
2.4. Optimization techniques in honeypots
Machine learning and AI have transformed honeypots into systems 
that can analyze huge amounts of data, identify patterns, and respond on 
a real-time basis. This enhances the detection capability and reduces 
manual effort in monitoring and analysis. For example, in the web-based 
honeypot in ( Jiang and Zheng, 2020 ), ensemble ML techniques are 
applied to filter out noise and focus on significant threats for improving 
the accuracy of detection. Similarly, Generative Adversarial Networks 
(GANs), applied in ( Ding, 2024 ), optimize honeypot responses, giving 
them greater effectiveness in resource-constrained environments. 
AI-powered honeypots largely improve the level of interaction by 
emulating human-like interactive capabilities that enable effective 
deception and intelligence gathering. Realistic responses of generative 
AI models in ( Raut et al., 2023 ), let honeypots dynamically learn from 
and adapt their tactics to attackers.
The deployment of honeypots is optimized using behavioral intelli -
gence from the SOAR engine ( Bartwal et al., 2022 ), analyzing attacker 
behaviors to guide strategic placement. When deployed in response to 
specific threats, these honeypots achieved longer engagement times and 
captured much more information about attacker tactics. These SOAR 
findings, in combination with the capabilities of LLMs, make this hon -
eypot especially relevant to modern contexts via the capability of per -
forming and dynamically updating responses in web API settings. The 
approaches described in ( Li et al., 2024 ) use RAG to increase the realism 
of honeypot interactions. The RAG-based approach retrieves 
pre-existing responses from a knowledge base and uses them as 
contextual inputs during the generation process, enabling the honeypot 
to correctly mimic the behaviors of legitimate APIs. Combining RAG 
with LLMs ensures that the responses of the honeypot are contextually 
accurate and semantically aligned, making it much more effective in 
engaging and deceiving attackers.
Reinforcement learning is now one of the essential tools in opti -
mizing honeypot functionality in dynamically adjusting the behaviors of 
an attacker, thereby further extending the durations of engagement. 
This adaptability enables the honeypot to harvest full intelligence about 
tactics employed by attackers and raises the utility value of use in the 
context of cybersecurity dramatically. In this context, ( Lopez-Yepez and 
Fagette, 2022 ) in the dynamic scenario, dynamic Secure Shell (SSH) 
honeypots make use of deep reinforcement learning methods for them to 
become sensitive, intelligently reacting to the behavior of detected at -
tackers. Not only does this customized approach increase the attacker’s 
time of interaction, but it also improves the honeypot ability of col -
lecting vital information and learning new attack schemes. Building on 
that idea, ( Gao et al., 2024 ) presented DShopping, an intelligent 
deception system to assist in fighting APTs by relying on exploiting static 
security configurations.
By using reinforcement learning, DSHopping is able to dynamically 
change the scenarios of deception for optimized engagement of attackers 
and real-time detection of APT behaviors. It underlines not only the 
synergy between machine learning and honeypot systems but also how 
they grow and react against complex and variable attack vectors. 
Through dynamic adaptation to adversaries’ strategies, the effectiveness 
of reinforcement learning—powered honeypots is enhanced several 
times over, while simultaneously contributing exponentially to cyber -
security intelligence. Hybrid AI architectures, fusing deep reinforcement 
learning, rule-based models, and LLMs, enable further evolution in 
reinforcing autonomous cybersecurity defense. These architectures, in 
their basic form, deploy agents in simulated environments that learn to 
interact with the main purpose of critical network protection by 
continuously enhancing their capability to predict attacks and response; 
an example of the adaptability of modern AI-driven honeypots. The 
incorporation of LLMs within such a hybrid architecture implies that 
honeypots can enhance the quality of response by better exploiting 
adaptive learning informed by intricate attack patterns ( Loevenich et al., 
2024 ).

more adaptable and scalable. For example, dynamic honeypots as in 
( Tang et al., 2023 ). apply deep learning on encrypted malicious traffic to 
offer better detection and engagement with sophisticated attackers
