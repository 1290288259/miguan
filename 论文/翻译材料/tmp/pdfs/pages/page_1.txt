ABSTRACT
As cyberattacks get more sophisticated, security systems must learn to detect and deceive them. DecoyPot, a 
honeypot Web Application Programming Interface (API) that generates legitimate API responses, is introduced in 
this paper. DecoyPot ’ s command extractor module carefully analyzes API requests to create prompt-response 
pairs that improve a Retrieval-Augmented Generation based (RAG) large language model (LLM). DecoyPot 
can instantly adjust its answers to mimic API activity in a contextually correct and convincing manner to at -
tackers. To assess system efficacy, we used a two-phase similarity analysis. Initial queries were matched with 
prompt-response pairs to ensure contextually suitable responses. Second, similarity measures were used to 
compare generated responses to reference responses, producing an average score of 0.9780. The high score shows 
that the system can create API-like responses, boosting its utility. DecoyPot engaged opponents and learned their 
Tactics, Techniques and Procedures (TTPs). The study shows that honeypot cybersecurity effectiveness must be 
improved by merging AI-driven response creation with enhanced deception technologies. DecoyPot effectively 
adapts to incoming queries and generates API-like responses, delivering actionable cyber threat intelligence and 
enhancing proactive defense strategies.
1. Introduction
Over the last couple of decades, the digital landscape has completely 
changed, and cyberattacks have increased in all sectors. These range 
from small organizations to critical infrastructure, exploiting vulnera -
bilities brought about by the proliferation of connected devices. This 
rapid growth of attack surfaces automatically demands innovation and 
adaptiveness in cybersecurity measures. One critical tool among these is 
the honeypot. Honeypots operate by emulating vulnerable systems to 
attract attackers and divert their focus from real targets, thereby 
revealing their techniques. This type of interaction generates valuable 
intelligence that informs the security strategy, discloses patterns of 
attack, and enables countermeasure development.
Cybersecurity honeypots have evolved significantly from their initial 
designs, which were limited to static systems aimed at identifying and 
logging basic network activity. While these early implementations pro -
vided valuable insights into basic attacker behaviors, they were insuf -
ficient for addressing sophisticated threats, such as advanced persistent 
threats (APTs). Modern honeypots have transitioned into dynamic and 
interactive systems that simulate realistic environments, enabling real- 
time engagement with attackers and capturing detailed information 
about their tactics, techniques, and procedures. Despite these advance -
ments, high-interaction honeypots present challenges, including 
increased complexity, resource demands, and the need for strict isola -
tion to avoid potential risks to production environments ( Kristyanto 
et al., 2022 ).
Despite their advancements, traditional honeypots face significant 
challenges in adapting to the rapidly evolving tactics of modern at -
tackers. Static or poorly configured systems are particularly vulnerable 
to detection, causing attackers to disengage or, worse, exploit the hon -
eypot to mislead defenders. High-interaction honeypots, while more 
effective at engaging attackers, generate large volumes of data that 
require time-intensive manual analysis, leading to response delays and 
missed opportunities for threat mitigation. Furthermore, many organi -
zations struggle to operationalize the intelligence gathered from these 
systems, limiting their overall utility ( Jiang and Zheng, 2020 ). Tradi -
tional honeypots often fail to attract sophisticated attackers, such as 
state-sponsored adversaries, due to their lack of dynamic interaction, 
which restricts their ability to gather actionable intelligence on attacker 
motivations and goals.
